Graphic Resume
My first attempt at creating a graphic resume

A little over a year ago, my friend had reached out to me about a possible open position and wanted me to send him my resume. I first checked with him to see if my resume was up to par and he proceeded to comb through it and tear it apart. I decided to totally rewrite it, but figured that this would be a good opportunity to make a graphic resume like I had wanted to for some time. Over the course of a week it went through several iterations until I landed on what I consider to be a pretty good balance between design and function.

Initial Design

When considering a graphical design for this resume, I wanted it to have some sort of personality and for it to not follow the typical hierarchical listing of skills and experience. When I first mocked it up on a white board I had broken the sections into skills/tools at the top, current projects in the middle, and relevant experience and interests at the bottom. This was illustrated with icons of various technologies floating around the head of a person holding a board with projects, all above a more standard section listing experience.

The transition from a hand-drawn mockup allowed me to get a better idea of positioning and color scheme. I was able to create the icons for various technologies and add a margin for personal info on the project board.

First Iteration

After a day or two, I had completed my first iteration. Aside from an official color scheme and better spacing and sizing of elements, the major change I made was to how the project board was to be presented. Rather than simply provide a title and icon with a descriptive list below it, I broke down each project into "The What" (which described the project) and "The How" (which described the tools used for the project). I thought that this was a good way to list skills while also giving them context.

The experience section was laid out pretty traditionally with it leading right into an awards/achievements and interests section. While I was proud of what I accomplished in such a short time, I knew that I had included some visual fluff with large dots that offered nothing to the design or flow and large, unnecessary margins.

Outside Perspective

Before settling on this version, I passed it on to some of my friends including one who has some graphic design experience. He addressed some of the issues I had felt self-conscious about and created a more blocked out version of the resume which proved to be much more ergonomic and easier to read.

Rather than a center aligned resume that took the reader down the middle of the page, a more traditional left-to-right orientation eliminated any initial confusion when it came to flow. The interests section was dropped and the awards/achievements section found a logical place as the footer.

Second Iteration

The second iteration introduced another big change to the projects board with a new, 3 column break down. I realized that a brief description and list of tools didn't tell the reader enough about the process. I introduced a "Learned" column along with renaming the others from "The What" to "Developed" and "The How" to "Used". This felt like a necessary improvement along with a more logical breakdown of a project.

Going off of the changes my friend had made, I chose a darker color scheme and highlighted the footer to distinguish the experience and awards/achievements sections. I also found that I still had room to incorporate the interests section and did so within a large red circle at the bottom center of the page. While I had not considered this design to be the final version, I felt comfortable enough to start applying to jobs.

Third and Final Iteration

At this point I had been using the previous version for a few months. I started to accumulate more experience and realized that there were certain aspects of the resume that were not necessary (the floating head and interests elements) or could be tightened up in terms of spacing.

After much tweaking, I was able to drop the floating head element and interests section and tighten the margins around each element to accommodate 3 more projects and 1 more work experience column. I also left-justified most of the content and removed unnecessary bolding throughout. The result is what I consider the current and final state of this style of resume.

Concept to Final Version

The resume came along way since the original hand-drawn and digital mockups. The initial design looks silly looking back at it, but it's interesting to see how the current version is clearly derived from it.

In the end, I think I was able to achieve the balance between visual design and function like I initially set out to do. The current design actually incorporates more content then I was able to fit on the Word document I had passed on to my friend before I started this project. What I value most about these unplanned projects however is the learning process and how the skills used gained from it will carry on to other future projects.
