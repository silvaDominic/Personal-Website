The Answer Company
A comprehensive website for custom print and promotional products

TheAnswerCompany.com is a completely custom site that strikes a balance between design and function. Pages maintain a logical, visual order while coming together to still boast a comprehensive catalog with over 40 pages. While I did not develop the original site, I have since taken the lead on the project, maintaining, expanding, and improving the site for the past year. You can check out the site over at TheAnswerCompany.com

Fine Tuning

In general, website development can broadly be broken down into two phases: the initial construction and the subsequent maintenance and scaling that follows. As the primary developer of the site, I often receive requests to add new elements, adjust styling, or update the content.

Two examples of a request for minor adjustments from the designer. On the left, a very subtle adjustment of padding to center align an element. On the right, a more detailed spec sheet for an existing element.

A slightly more comprehensive example that involves changing a header layout and incorporating some buttons. Some elements are disregarded altogether.

While generally as simple as it sounds, maintaining responsiveness with new elements can be the bulk of the work...

Making sure the 4 nav options fit at the top and scaled without overlapping took hours to perfect.

A closer look at the different elements used for button states for the top nav bar.

Scaling Up

In addition to the ever going 'fine tuning' of the site, there are larger projects that require the creation of new elements, pages, and features.

The evolution of a mail tout element. An entirely new element is first created and then adjusted according to the designer's preferences.

Depending on the type of element, several versions of an element need to be created to accommodate multiple views.

More examples of custom elements and their respective versions.

A more comprehensive addition to the site involved adding a new 'hub' element to each major section, consolidating much of the content and expanding the site by more than 20 pages.

Another example of element specifications used for responsive design is depicted on the right.

A side by side comparison of the old and new landing page. Element arrangement of the side bar and the addition of a button at the bottom of the hub are also implemented.

While the creation of a hub gave the user better control of where to explore, they still could only reach it by navigating to the parent page first. The obvious solution was to expand the menu to include these options.

A side by side comparison illustrates how the old menu only contains the parent page options (left) while the new and expanded menu features parent and sub-page options (right)

Aside from my own website, I hadn't really appreciated the second phase of website building. I would always get requests from old clients for advice or an occasional tweak, but I hadn't collaborated with a web designer on a project to project basis like I did with The Answer Company. This communication along with the slower pace of maintaining and expanding a site over time was a good step in elevating my experience as a developer.
