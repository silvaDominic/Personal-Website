So right before I graduated college in 2014 I told myself that by 2016 I'd be calling Australia my home, working for some renewable energy company on the weekdays, enjoying the Gold Coast on the weekends, and melting to the distinct sound of those Aussie accents everywhere in between......HA! Considering that I'm typing this from the bedroom I grew up in, I think that I may have really screwed up somewhere. Well actually, that's what you would be thinking. Rather than compare my current situation to that ambitious *cough* silly *cough* 'promise' I made to myself a couple of years ago, instead I think of it as if nature took my hand, gave me a smack, and whispered into my soul in a not so sweet Australian voice "Oye, I don't think Australia is ready for you mate." Maybe I had been hyping things up a bit. "...You might want to reconsider your career too." Oh come on!

I think that change of direction stemmed from a gut feeling I got when I considered my future as a body and soul rather than just another member of society. Life in Australia as an engineer certainly sounds appealing (I'll travel there at some point), but I think I had a deep desire to express myself through other means and I didn't see how taking that path would allow for such a thing. For a while I thought about following up on the dream I had when I was younger and becoming a game developer. After a summer of couch hopping, working at a restaurant, conversations with friends and family, and self reflecting, I decided that I would learn how to program in order to do just that. What made game development an easy choice didn't so much have to do with me growing up with video games, but that creating them incorporated a lot of other disciplines that I'm interested in: design, digital art, photography, music, and storytelling. These were the things that I felt would truly allow me to express myself. But as my self study progressed and I got closer and closer to attaining a job, I thought more about what I would actually do as a professional. "What kinds of games would I make?", "Who would these games be for?", "Would I even get to work on the music? Or the story?" I eventually realized that I would not have the chance to explore these things as long as I was under someone else's umbrella and that the only way I could pursue them is if I made the games myself. A shattered dream once more? Eh, more like a new perspective...But now what do I do with all of these programmer skills?

Currently, the plan is open ended. I enjoy putting my web design and photography skills to use on freelance projects, but I am also still making an effort to merge my traditional engineering skills with my software ones in order to work in a more design oriented field. Part of me feels like I'm back to square one, still contemplating the 'long road ahead', but the other part of me can't help but appreciate all that I've learned over these past 2 years. This road may not end soon, but with new talent and a new perspective, I've got a much better idea of where to go.

My interest in photography and photo editing developed after the release of Halo 3 in 2007. The game featured a 'Theater Mode' that allowed players to rewatch their gameplay experiences and snap pictures of pretty much whatever they liked. As my library expanded, I eventually began to explore photo and video editing capturing landscapes and moments from the game world that Halo 3 offered. From there I moved to my mom's digital camera, then to my friend's DSLR in college, and eventually to my own DSLR recently. I've taken thousands of pictures since my initial interest back in 2007 and have selected a few of albums to share here. You'll find my recently accumulated work ranging from intimate nature shots to candid views of a bustling port town.
