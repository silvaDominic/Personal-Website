I'm sort of new at this...

So right before I graduated college in 2014 I told myself that by 2016 I'd be calling Australia my home, working for some renewable energy company on the weekdays, enjoying the Gold Coast on the weekends, and melting to the distinct sound of those Aussie accents......HA! Considering that I'm typing this from the bedroom I grew up in, I think that I may have really screwed up somewhere. Well actually, that's what you would be thinking. Rather than compare my current situation to that ambitious *cough* silly *cough* 'promise' I made to myself a couple of years ago, instead I think of it as if nature took my hand, gave me a smack, and whispered into my soul in a not so sweet Australian voice "Oye, I don't think Australia is ready for you mate." Maybe I had been hyping things up a bit. "...You might want to reconsider your career too." Oh come on!

I think that change of direction stemmed from a gut feeling I got when I considered my future as a body and soul rather than just another member of society. Life in Australia as an engineer certainly sounds appealing (I'll travel there at some point, I'm sure), but I think I had a deep desire to express myself through other means and I didn't see how taking that path would allow for such a thing. It was around this time that I also started to seriously consider a dream of mine that I deemed totally unrealistic right before I went off to college: becoming a game a developer. After a summer of couch hopping, working at a restaurant, conversations with friends and family, and self reflecting I decided that I wanted to learn software development and design in order to [ultimately] become just that. What made game development an easy choice didn't so much have to do with me growing up with video games, but that creating them incorporated a lot of other disciplines that I'm interested in: design, digital art, photography, music, and storytelling. These were the things that I felt would truly allow me to express myself. And while there is a video game stigma among the older generations (I'm talking to the parents out there), it should be known that it's also because of these things that video games have the ability to be wholesome projects that require more than just quick reflexes and a sharp memory.

It's been nearly a year and a half since I've made that decision and I've gotta say, I feel much more confident about the switch now than I did then. My conceptual 'physique' is still slim however, and it's at this point that I think moving into, what do they call it? the “real world”, is necessary to solidify the core computer science and programming principles I've learned and to start becoming familiar with the more nuanced ones. With this website finally up and running, I think I'm one step closer to achieving that.

My interest in photography and photo editing developed after the release of Halo 3 in 2007. The game featured a 'Theater Mode' that allowed players to rewatch their gameplay experiences and snap pictures if they liked. After about a year I had reached the photo limit for my account. From there I moved to my mom's digital camera, then to my friend's DSLR in college, and eventually to my own DSLR recently. I've taken thousands of pictures since my initial interest back in 2007 and have selected a few of my favorite albums to share here. You'll find my recently accumulated work ranging from intimate nature shots to candid views of a bustling port town.
